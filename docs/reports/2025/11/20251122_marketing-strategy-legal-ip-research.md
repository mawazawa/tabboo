## 2. Ghostbusters Jingle Legal Analysis

### The Legal Landscape is HOSTILE

#### Blurred Lines Case (2015) - The New Standard

The Pharrell Williams / Robin Thicke v. Marvin Gaye estate case fundamentally changed music copyright law:

- **Verdict**: $5.3 million in damages + 50% of future royalties
- **Key Ruling**: The jury found infringement based on the **"feel"** of the rhythm section, even though:
  - No melodies were copied
  - No lyrics were copied
  - The songs were in different keys

> "The judgment establishes a dangerous precedent that strikes a devastating blow to future musicians and composers everywhere." — Judge Nguyen (dissent)

**Implication for DocBusters**: You cannot safely create a "Ghostbusters-inspired" jingle that captures the "feel" even without copying notes.

#### Sound-Alike Advertising Cases

Recent case law shows HIGH risk for commercial sound-alikes:

1. **Eminem v. New Zealand National Party (2017)**: A political party used licensed stock music called "Eminem Esque" that was a sound-alike of "Lose Yourself." Even though they properly licensed the stock piece, they were found guilty and fined **$600,000 NZD**.

2. **Bette Midler v. Ford Motor Company (1988)**: Ad agency recreated one of her hits using a sound-alike singer. Midler won on "right of publicity" grounds.

3. **The Black Keys v. Pizza Hut**: Sued over sound-alike ads.

**Key Legal Principle**:
> "An advertiser who uses music which sounds like an 'essential air or melody' of part of another song will run a serious risk of being found liable for copyright infringement."

#### Parody Defense - NOT VIABLE for Commercial Use

While parody can be fair use (Campbell v. Acuff-Rose Music, 1994), it requires:
- Commentary on the ORIGINAL work itself
- NOT just using a catchy melody with new lyrics
- Courts are more skeptical of commercial/advertising use

> "In order to be covered under a fair use exception you will have to actually parody the work itself, NOT just write your own funny lyrics to someone else's music."

**DocBusters is NOT a parody of Ghostbusters** - it's using the cultural reference to sell a different product. This fails the parody test.

#### Ghostbusters-Specific Issues

1. **Trademark**: GHOSTBUSTERS is heavily trademarked by Columbia Pictures Industries, Inc. (multiple registrations covering entertainment, toys, games, etc.)

2. **Music Rights**: The song is owned by Sony/ATV Tunes LLC

3. **Historical Litigation**: The Ghostbusters theme itself was subject to a lawsuit:
   - Huey Lewis sued Ray Parker Jr. for plagiarism (settled out of court, 1985)
   - The filmmakers admitted using Lewis's "I Want a New Drug" as temp music and giving it to Parker
   - Parker later sued Lewis for breaching confidentiality agreement

4. **Sony's Enforcement**: Per discussions with Sony's litigation department:
   > "They will go after anyone who is profiting from copyright usage, or anyone who is besmirching their copyright in an unsavoury manner."

### Jingle Options (Ranked by Risk)

| Option | Risk Level | Legal Analysis |
|--------|-----------|----------------|
| **License the actual Ghostbusters theme** | LOW | Contact Sony/Columbia Pictures licensing. Expensive but safe. |
| **Create original music with no similarity** | LOW | Hire a composer to create completely original jingle. |
| **Sound-alike that captures "feel"** | EXTREME | Per Blurred Lines, even "feel" similarity is infringement. |
| **Parody with same melody/new lyrics** | HIGH | Not true parody (not commenting on Ghostbusters). Commercial use. |
| **Transformative 3-4 note "wink"** | HIGH | No "number of notes" rule exists. Substantial similarity test applies. |

### Recommendation: AVOID Ghostbusters Musical Reference

**The math doesn't work:**
- Cost to license: Likely $50,000-$500,000+ (if they even agree)
- Cost of lawsuit: $500,000-$5,000,000+
- Risk of losing: HIGH (post-Blurred Lines)

**Better Approach**: Create an ORIGINAL jingle that is memorable on its own merits. The DocBusters NAME already provides the cultural wink - you don't need the music too.

**Prior Art Distinction**: OCR solutions provide raw text. LLM solutions require prompting. SwiftFill combines both stages with legal-document-specific structured output.
